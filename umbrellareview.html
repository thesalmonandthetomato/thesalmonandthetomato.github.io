The Salmon and the Tomato

by Neal Haddaway, María D López Rodríguez, Hazel Cooley

The state of evidence on salmon farming:
an umbrella review


This literature review analysed 37 systematic reviews on salmon farming, assessing their scope, quality, and focus across environmental, economic, and social impacts. Despite a boom in salmon research, most reviews barely scratch the surface, focusing on production while ignoring wider environmental and social costs. High-quality, transparent, and independent systematic reviews are urgently needed to reveal the true impacts of the global salmon industry.

Why this review matters

Salmon farming has exploded worldwide, reshaping marine food systems and driving huge economic gains. But this growth comes with serious questions: what are the environmental costs, how are local communities affected, and who benefits from the research itself?

Most existing studies focus narrowly on production issues like fish health and feed, leaving the broader social and ecological impacts largely unexplored. Without high-quality, independent evidence, policymakers and the public lack the tools to make informed decisions about the future of salmon aquaculture.

Key takeaway: The industry is growing faster than our understanding of its true impacts, making rigorous, unbiased research more important than ever.

What we did

We conducted an umbrella review of existing systematic reviews on salmon aquaculture to assess what is known, what is missing, and how reliable the evidence base really is. Using a structured and pre-registered search strategy, we screened 327 records from major academic databases and included 37 reviews focused on salmon farming from around the world.

Each review was analysed for its topical focus, production stage, and funding sources, and critically appraised using the CEESAT tool to assess methodological rigour. We then mapped the evidence visually to identify patterns, gaps, and potential biases across environmental, economic, and social impacts.

Key findings

A large but weak evidence base

Out of 327 records screened, only 37 systematic reviews met the inclusion criteria. None were assessed as high quality, and just four reached a moderate standard of methodological rigour. Most reviews lacked core elements of robust evidence synthesis, including pre-registered protocols, transparent screening methods, and critical appraisal.

A strong bias towards production concerns

The evidence base is heavily skewed towards production-related topics such as fish health, sea lice, and feed supply. Environmental impacts and social outcomes are rarely addressed, leaving major knowledge gaps around pollution, ecosystem effects, labour conditions, and community impacts.

Methodological flaws are widespread

Common weaknesses include poor reporting of methods, absence of bias assessment, and inappropriate synthesis approaches such as vote counting. These limitations reduce confidence in many widely cited conclusions about salmon farming sustainability.

Industry influence is visible

Seven reviews included industry co-authors, and eight reported industry funding. In many cases, funding sources or conflicts of interest were poorly reported, raising concerns about transparency and agenda-setting within the review literature.
